Every year, someone asks me: *"Why are you still using SSR markers? Just do WGS."*

It is a fair question. Whole-genome sequencing costs have dropped dramatically. For large programs with sufficient funding, SNP arrays and WGS do offer unmatched resolution.

But for breeding programs like the ones I work with at BARI — and for most programs in low- and middle-income countries — SSRs remain irreplaceable.

## The Case for SSRs

**1. Cost per sample is orders of magnitude lower**

Running 20 SSR markers across 200 accessions is achievable with a modest budget. WGS of those same 200 samples is not. In resource-limited settings, this is the decisive factor.

**2. Interpretability**

SSR data — allele sizes, heterozygosity, PIC values — are interpretable by any geneticist with a standard analysis workflow. GenAlEx, PowerMarker, STRUCTURE. No bioinformatics pipeline required.

**3. Robustness of existing marker sets**

For species like chrysanthemum and apple, there are validated SSR panels with known PIC values, genomic locations, and allele frequency databases. Starting from scratch with WGS takes years of validation.

**4. Diagnostic precision for diversity studies**

For the question "how genetically diverse is this collection?" — SSRs with high PIC values give you a defensible answer. You don't always need the genome.

## When WGS Wins

I'm not arguing against WGS. For:

- Fine mapping QTLs
- GWAS with thousands of accessions
- Understanding structural variation
- Programs with sequencing infrastructure

WGS is clearly superior.

## A Practical Synthesis

The best programs I've seen combine both: SSRs for initial diversity characterization, germplasm management, and routine fingerprinting — WGS when fine-resolution questions arise.

For my current work on apple breeding, SSR and SCAR markers gave us exactly what we needed: population structure, genetic distance, and selection targets — all within a realistic budget and timeline.

The tools should match the question and the resources. That's the principle I work by.
